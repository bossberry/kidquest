# Division Readiness

## Purpose

Prepare children for division by developing the ideas of equal sharing, grouping, skip counting, and the relationship between multiplication and division.

---

## Core Concepts

Division means:

- sharing equally
- making groups
- finding how many groups
- finding how many are in each group

Division readiness begins long before children learn the division symbol (÷).

## Learning Progression

### Stage 1: Equal Sharing

Example:

6 cookies shared between 2 children.

🍪🍪🍪🍪🍪🍪

Each child gets:

🍪🍪🍪

Answer:

3 each

---

### Stage 2: Making Equal Groups

Example:

8 blocks

Make groups of 2.

🟦🟦 | 🟦🟦 | 🟦🟦 | 🟦🟦

Answer:

4 groups

---

### Stage 3: Repeated Subtraction

Example:

10 candies

Remove groups of 2.

10 → 8 → 6 → 4 → 2 → 0

Five groups

Answer:

5

---

### Stage 4: Skip Counting

Count by:

- 2s
- 5s
- 10s

Examples:

2, 4, 6, 8, 10

5, 10, 15, 20

10, 20, 30, 40

Skip counting builds multiplication and division fluency.

---

### Stage 5: Arrays

Example:

12 objects arranged:

● ● ● ●

● ● ● ●

● ● ● ●

Three rows of four

or

Four columns of three

Total:

12

---

### Stage 6: Multiplication Relationship

Example:

3 × 4 = 12

Therefore:

12 ÷ 4 = 3

12 ÷ 3 = 4

Children begin to see inverse operations.

---

### Stage 7: Remainders

Example:

7 cookies shared among 2 children.

Each receives:

3 cookies

One cookie remains.

Answer:

3 remainder 1

---

## Equal Sharing vs Grouping

### Sharing

8 candies shared among 4 children.

Question:

How many for each child?

Answer:

2

---

### Grouping

8 candies arranged into groups of 2.

Question:

How many groups?

Answer:

4

Both situations involve division but require different thinking.

---

## Common Misconceptions

### Sharing Unequally

Incorrect:

One child gets more.

Correct:

Division requires equal groups.

---

### Confusing Groups and Items

Example:

12 objects in groups of 3.

Incorrect answer:

3

Correct answer:

4 groups

---

### Assuming There Is Always No Remainder

Some numbers do not divide evenly.

Example:

10 ÷ 3

Answer:

3 remainder 1

### Beginner

6 shared by 3

---

### Intermediate

Grouping within 20.

Examples:

12 grouped by 4

15 grouped by 5

---

### Advanced

Including remainders.

Examples:

17 shared by 5

19 shared by 4

---

## Teacher Notes

Focus on concrete experiences before introducing symbols.

Emphasize:

- fairness
- equal groups
- visual models

Avoid memorizing division facts too early.

---

## Parent Notes

Use everyday situations.

Examples:

- sharing snacks
- dividing toys
- arranging seats
- packing groceries

Ask:

"How many groups?"

"How many in each group?"
