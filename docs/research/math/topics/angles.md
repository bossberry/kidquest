# Angles

## Purpose

Help children understand turns and rotations, building the foundation for geometry, shapes, trigonometry, and spatial reasoning.

---

## Core Concepts

An angle measures the amount of turn between two rays.

Angles are formed when two lines meet at a common point.

Examples:

- corners of shapes
- clock hands
- opening doors

Angles measure turning, not length.

## Learning Progression

### Stage 1: Understanding Turns

Children recognize:

- quarter turn
- half turn
- full turn

Examples:

Turning wheels

Opening doors

Clock hands

---

### Stage 2: Recognizing Angles

Examples:

Corners of:

- books
- windows
- tables

Children identify where two sides meet.

---

### Stage 3: Right Angles

Right angles measure 90°.

Examples:

- squares
- rectangles

Right angles are common in buildings and furniture.

---

### Stage 4: Acute Angles

Acute angles are less than 90°.

Examples:

30°

45°

60°

Acute angles are smaller than right angles.

---

### Stage 5: Obtuse Angles

Obtuse angles are greater than 90° and less than 180°.

Examples:

100°

120°

150°

Obtuse angles are wider than right angles.

---

### Stage 6: Straight Angles

Straight angles measure 180°.

Example:

A straight line.

---

### Stage 7: Full Turns

A complete rotation measures 360°.

Examples:

Wheel rotation

Clock hands

---

### Stage 8: Measuring Angles

Use a protractor to measure degrees.

Example:

70°

120°

90°

---

## Common Misconceptions

### Bigger Sides Mean Bigger Angles

Angle size depends on turning, not side length.

---

### Vertical Position Determines Angle Size

Rotating an angle does not change its measure.

---

### Acute Means Small Shape

Acute describes angle measure, not object size.

---

### Angles Require Horizontal Lines

Angles can appear in any orientation.
