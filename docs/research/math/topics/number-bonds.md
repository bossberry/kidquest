# Number Bonds

## Purpose

Help children understand how numbers can be composed and decomposed into smaller parts, forming the foundation for addition, subtraction, mental math, and place value.

---

## Core Concepts

A number bond shows how a whole number can be split into parts.

Example:

Whole:

5

Parts:

2 and 3

Therefore:

2 + 3 = 5

5 − 2 = 3

5 − 3 = 2

## Learning Progression

### Stage 1: Parts and Whole

Example:

Whole:

4

Possible parts:

1 and 3

2 and 2

0 and 4

Children understand that numbers can be broken apart in different ways.

---

### Stage 2: Number Bonds Within 5

Examples:

5

- 0 + 5
- 1 + 4
- 2 + 3

---

### Stage 3: Number Bonds Within 10

Examples:

10

- 1 + 9
- 2 + 8
- 3 + 7
- 4 + 6
- 5 + 5

These bonds support make-ten strategies.

---

### Stage 4: Fact Families

Example:

Parts:

4 and 3

Whole:

7

Related facts:

4 + 3 = 7

3 + 4 = 7

7 − 4 = 3

7 − 3 = 4

---

### Stage 5: Decomposing Numbers

Example:

8

Can be split as:

5 + 3

6 + 2

7 + 1

4 + 4

Children learn that there are multiple ways to represent a number.

---

### Stage 6: Make Ten Strategy

Example:

8 + 5

Break apart 5:

2 + 3

8 + 2 = 10

10 + 3 = 13

Number bonds help children solve problems efficiently.

---

### Stage 7: Larger Number Bonds

Example:

20

Possible parts:

10 + 10

12 + 8

15 + 5

18 + 2

---

## Fact Families

Example:

Whole:

9

Parts:

6 and 3

Equations:

6 + 3 = 9

3 + 6 = 9

9 − 6 = 3

9 − 3 = 6

Fact families show the relationship between addition and subtraction.

---

## Common Misconceptions

### A Number Has Only One Pair

Incorrect:

10 = 5 + 5 only

Correct:

10 can be represented in many ways.

---

### Number Order Matters

Incorrect:

2 + 3 is different from 3 + 2

Correct:

Both equal 5.

---

### Forgetting Zero

Examples:

7

0 + 7

7 + 0

Zero is an important part of number bonds.

---

### Memorizing Without Understanding

Children should understand why the parts make the whole.

## Teacher Notes

Number bonds are more important than memorizing facts.

Encourage flexible thinking.

Develop understanding before speed.

Use visual models frequently.

---

## Parent Notes

Practice naturally.

Examples:

"We have 8 strawberries."

"If I eat 3, how many are left?"

"How can we split 10 crackers equally?"

Keep activities playful and conversational.
