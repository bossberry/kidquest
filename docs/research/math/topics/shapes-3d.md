# 3D Shapes

## Purpose

Help children recognize, describe, compare, and classify three-dimensional objects, building the foundation for geometry, measurement, volume, and spatial reasoning.

---

## Core Concepts

Three-dimensional (3D) shapes are solid objects.

They have:

- length
- width
- height

Unlike 2D shapes, 3D shapes occupy space.

Examples:

- cube
- sphere
- cylinder
- cone

## Learning Progression

### Stage 1: Recognizing Everyday Objects

Children identify common solids.

Examples:

- ball
- box
- can
- ice cream cone

---

### Stage 2: Naming 3D Shapes

Examples:

- cube
- sphere
- cylinder
- cone

---

### Stage 3: Identifying Attributes

Children describe:

- faces
- edges
- vertices
- curved surfaces

---

### Stage 4: Comparing Shapes

Examples:

Cube vs Sphere

Cone vs Cylinder

Children identify similarities and differences.

---

### Stage 5: Matching 3D Shapes to Real Objects

Examples:

Cube → dice

Sphere → basketball

Cylinder → soda can

Cone → traffic cone

---

### Stage 6: Sorting Shapes

Group solids by:

- number of faces
- curved surfaces
- ability to roll

---

### Stage 7: Understanding Nets

Children explore how flat shapes fold into solids.

Examples:

Cube nets

Cylinder nets

## Common 3D Shapes

### Cube

Properties:

- 6 square faces
- 12 edges
- 8 vertices

Examples:

- dice
- toy blocks

---

### Rectangular Prism

Properties:

- 6 rectangular faces
- 12 edges
- 8 vertices

Examples:

- cereal box
- book

---

### Sphere

Properties:

- no edges
- no vertices
- one curved surface

Examples:

- soccer ball
- orange

---

### Cylinder

Properties:

- 2 circular faces
- one curved surface

Examples:

- soda can
- candle

---

### Cone

Properties:

- one circular base
- one vertex
- one curved surface

Examples:

- ice cream cone
- party hat

---

### Pyramid

Properties:

- triangular faces meet at one vertex

Examples:

- Egyptian pyramids

---

## Rolling, Stacking, and Sliding

### Roll

Shapes with curved surfaces.

Examples:

- sphere
- cylinder
- cone

---

### Stack

Shapes with flat faces.

Examples:

- cube
- rectangular prism

---

### Slide

Shapes with flat surfaces.

Examples:

- box
- book

---

## Relationship Between 2D and 3D Shapes

Examples:

Cube → square faces

Cylinder → circular faces

Cone → circular base

Understanding this connection supports geometry and volume.

---

## Common Misconceptions

### Size Changes the Shape

Large and small spheres are both spheres.

---

### All Round Objects Are Spheres

Example:

A soda can is a cylinder, not a sphere.

---

### Confusing Faces and Edges

Children may count incorrectly.

Concrete models help clarify attributes.

---

### Orientation Changes the Shape

Turning a cube does not create a new shape.
