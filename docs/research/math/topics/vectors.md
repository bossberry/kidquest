# Vectors

## Purpose

Help children understand quantities that have both magnitude and direction, building the foundation for physics, engineering, computer graphics, and linear algebra.

---

## Core Concepts

Some quantities only have size.

Examples:

- length
- mass
- temperature

These are called scalars.

Other quantities have both:

- magnitude
- direction

These are called vectors.

Examples:

- velocity
- force
- displacement

Vectors describe movement and direction.

## Learning Progression

### Stage 1: Direction

Examples:

- north
- south
- east
- west

Movement requires both distance and direction.

---

### Stage 2: Displacement

Example:

Walk:

5 meters east

Distance:

5 meters

Direction:

east

Together they form a vector.

---

### Stage 3: Representing Vectors

Vectors are often drawn as arrows.

The arrow indicates:

- length → magnitude
- orientation → direction

---

### Stage 4: Comparing Scalars and Vectors

Scalar:

10 kg

Vector:

10 m/s east

Vectors require direction.

---

### Stage 5: Components

A vector may be separated into:

- horizontal component
- vertical component

Example:

Moving northeast involves both directions.

---

### Stage 6: Vector Addition

Example:

3 units right

plus

2 units up

produces a new vector.

---

### Stage 7: Resultant Vectors

Combining vectors produces a resultant vector.

Example:

Wind and airplane velocity combine.

---

### Stage 8: Coordinates and Vectors

Vectors can be represented as:

(x, y)

Example:

(3,4)

## Common Misconceptions

### Vectors Are Just Numbers

Vectors include direction.

---

### Negative Values Mean Smaller Magnitudes

Negative coordinates indicate direction.

---

### Distance and Displacement Are Identical

Distance ignores direction.

Displacement includes direction.

---

### Arrows Represent Size Only

Arrows represent both size and direction.
