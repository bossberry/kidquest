# Exponents and Powers

## Purpose

Help children understand repeated multiplication and patterns of growth, building the foundation for algebra, scientific notation, functions, and advanced mathematics.

---

## Core Concepts

Exponents provide a compact way to represent repeated multiplication.

Example:

2 × 2 × 2 × 2

can be written as:

2⁴

where:

- 2 is the base
- 4 is the exponent

Exponents describe repeated multiplication, not repeated addition.

## Learning Progression

### Stage 1: Repeated Multiplication

Example:

3 × 3 × 3

Three factors of 3.

---

### Stage 2: Introducing Exponents

Example:

3³

means:

3 × 3 × 3

Answer:

27

---

### Stage 3: Squares

Example:

5²

means:

5 × 5

Answer:

25

Squares represent area models.

---

### Stage 4: Cubes

Example:

4³

means:

4 × 4 × 4

Answer:

64

Cubes relate to volume.

---

### Stage 5: Powers of Ten

Examples:

10¹ = 10

10² = 100

10³ = 1,000

Patterns become visible.

---

### Stage 6: Comparing Exponential Growth

Example:

2² = 4

2³ = 8

2⁴ = 16

Values increase rapidly.

---

### Stage 7: Zero Exponent

Example:

5⁰ = 1

Any nonzero number raised to the zero power equals 1.

---

### Stage 8: Negative Exponents

Example:

2⁻¹

represents:

1/2

Negative exponents indicate reciprocals.

## Common Misconceptions

### Exponents Mean Multiplication by the Exponent

Incorrect:

3⁴ = 12

Correct:

3 × 3 × 3 × 3 = 81

---

### Zero Exponent Means Zero

Incorrect:

5⁰ = 0

Correct:

5⁰ = 1

---

### Negative Exponents Produce Negative Numbers

Negative exponents indicate reciprocals, not negative values.

---

### 2³ Means 2 × 3

Incorrect.

2³ means:

2 × 2 × 2
