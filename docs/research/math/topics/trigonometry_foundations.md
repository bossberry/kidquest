# Trigonometry Foundations

## Purpose

Help children understand the relationships between angles and side lengths in triangles, building the foundation for geometry, physics, engineering, navigation, and calculus.

---

## Core Concepts

Trigonometry studies the relationships between:

- angles
- side lengths

Trigonometry is based primarily on right triangles.

The three fundamental ratios are:

- sine
- cosine
- tangent

These ratios connect geometry and algebra.

## Learning Progression

### Stage 1: Review of Angles

Examples:

30°

45°

90°

Angles measure rotation.

---

### Stage 2: Right Triangles

A right triangle contains one 90° angle.

Right triangles are the basis of trigonometry.

---

### Stage 3: Identifying Sides

For a given angle:

- opposite side
- adjacent side
- hypotenuse

depend on the reference angle.

---

### Stage 4: Understanding Ratios

Ratios compare side lengths.

Example:

Opposite side / Hypotenuse

---

### Stage 5: Sine

Sine relates:

Opposite side

to

Hypotenuse

---

### Stage 6: Cosine

Cosine relates:

Adjacent side

to

Hypotenuse

---

### Stage 7: Tangent

Tangent relates:

Opposite side

to

Adjacent side

---

### Stage 8: Solving Right Triangles

Knowing:

- one angle
- one side

allows unknown lengths to be found.

---

## Parts of a Right Triangle

### Hypotenuse

The longest side.

Opposite the 90° angle.

---

### Opposite Side

Across from the reference angle.

---

### Adjacent Side

Next to the reference angle.

---

## Trigonometric Ratios

### Sine

Opposite / Hypotenuse

---

### Cosine

Adjacent / Hypotenuse

---

### Tangent

Opposite / Adjacent

---

## Relationship with Similar Triangles

Trigonometric ratios remain constant for similar triangles.

Example:

30° triangles always produce the same sine value.

---

## Relationship with Circles

Trigonometry extends beyond triangles to circles.

The unit circle becomes important in advanced mathematics.

## Common Misconceptions

### The Longest Side Is Always the Base

The hypotenuse is determined by the right angle, not orientation.

---

### Opposite and Adjacent Never Change

They depend on the reference angle.

---

### Trigonometry Applies Only to Triangles

It later extends to circles and functions.

---

### Bigger Angles Mean Bigger Triangles

Angle size and triangle size are independent.
