# Estimation

## Purpose

Help children develop number sense and reasonable thinking by learning to make approximate answers without needing exact calculations.

---

## Core Concepts

Estimation means finding a value that is close to the exact answer.

Estimates are useful when:

- exact answers are unnecessary
- checking whether answers make sense
- making quick decisions

Examples:

48 is about 50.

19 + 22 is about 20 + 20 = 40.

## Learning Progression

### Stage 1: More or Less

Compare quantities.

Examples:

Which group has more?

Which group has fewer?

Children begin developing intuitive quantity sense.

---

### Stage 2: Visual Estimation

Guess the number of objects before counting.

Examples:

- marbles
- stickers
- blocks

Then compare the estimate with the actual quantity.

---

### Stage 3: Benchmarks

Children use familiar reference points.

Examples:

- 10 fingers
- 5 days of school
- 12 eggs in a carton

Benchmarks improve estimation accuracy.

---

### Stage 4: Rounding Numbers

Examples:

23 ≈ 20

48 ≈ 50

71 ≈ 70

Children learn to round to the nearest ten.

---

### Stage 5: Estimating Sums

Example:

27 + 34

Round:

30 + 30

Estimate:

60

Exact answer:

61

---

### Stage 6: Estimating Differences

Example:

83 − 39

Round:

80 − 40

Estimate:

40

Exact answer:

44

---

### Stage 7: Estimating Products

Example:

19 × 5

Round:

20 × 5

Estimate:

100

Exact answer:

95

---

### Stage 8: Estimating Measurements

Examples:

Length:

The table is about 1 meter long.

Weight:

The bag weighs about 2 kilograms.

Capacity:

The bottle holds about 1 liter.

---

## Front-End Estimation

Estimate before calculating.

Example:

48 + 31

Think:

40 + 30

Estimate:

70

Exact answer:

79

---

## Compatible Numbers

Choose numbers that are easy to calculate.

Example:

198 + 101

Think:

200 + 100

Estimate:

300

Exact answer:

299

---

## Common Misconceptions

### Estimation Means Guessing Randomly

Incorrect.

Estimation uses reasoning and benchmarks.

---

### Estimates Must Be Exact

Estimates are intended to be close, not perfect.

---

### Rounding Always Gives the Same Answer

Different rounding strategies may produce slightly different estimates.

---

### Ignoring Whether an Answer Makes Sense

Estimation is useful for checking unreasonable answers.

Example:

27 + 15 = 412

Clearly unreasonable.

---

## Teacher Notes

Encourage children to explain why their estimates are reasonable.

Avoid treating estimation as careless guessing.

Develop number sense rather than speed.

---

## Parent Notes

Use estimation naturally.

Examples:

- How many grapes are left?
- About how long will the trip take?
- How much does this bag weigh?

Praise reasonable thinking, not perfect accuracy.
