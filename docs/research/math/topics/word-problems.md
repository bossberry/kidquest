# Word Problems

## Purpose

Help children translate real-world situations into mathematical thinking and understand that mathematics is used to solve meaningful problems.

---

## Core Concepts

Word problems require children to:

1. Understand the story.
2. Identify quantities.
3. Determine relationships.
4. Choose an operation.
5. Solve the problem.
6. Check whether the answer makes sense.

Word problems are about reasoning, not keyword memorization.

## Learning Progression

### Stage 1: Concrete Story Problems

Example:

Tom has 2 apples.

Mom gives him 3 more apples.

How many apples does Tom have now?

Answer:

5

Use objects and pictures.

---

### Stage 2: Picture-Based Problems

Children use drawings to represent quantities.

Example:

🐻🐻🐻

Add:

🐻🐻

Total:

5 bears

---

### Stage 3: One-Step Problems

Use one operation.

Examples:

Addition

7 + 4

Subtraction

10 − 3

Multiplication

4 × 2

Division

12 ÷ 3

---

### Stage 4: Missing Number Problems

Example:

Sarah has 8 stickers.

She gives some away.

Now she has 5 stickers.

How many stickers did she give away?

Answer:

3

---

### Stage 5: Two-Step Problems

Example:

There are 5 red balloons and 3 blue balloons.

Two balloons pop.

How many balloons remain?

Step 1:

5 + 3 = 8

Step 2:

8 − 2 = 6

Answer:

6

---

## Problem Types

### Join Problems

Something is added.

Example:

3 birds sit on a tree.

2 more birds arrive.

How many birds are there now?

Answer:

5

---

### Separate Problems

Something is removed.

Example:

8 cookies are on a plate.

3 are eaten.

How many remain?

Answer:

5

---

### Part-Part-Whole Problems

Combine parts to make a whole.

Example:

4 boys and 6 girls.

How many children altogether?

Answer:

10

---

### Compare Problems

Compare two quantities.

Example:

Anna has 9 pencils.

Ben has 6 pencils.

How many more pencils does Anna have?

Answer:

3

---

### Equal Groups Problems

Example:

There are 3 bags.

Each bag contains 4 oranges.

How many oranges are there?

Answer:

12

---

### Sharing Problems

Example:

12 candies are shared equally among 4 children.

How many candies does each child receive?

Answer:

3

---

## Unknown Position Problems

### Unknown Result

5 + 3 = ?

Answer:

8

---

### Unknown Change

5 + ? = 9

Answer:

4

---

### Unknown Start

? + 4 = 10

Answer:

6

These are often more challenging than unknown-result problems.

## Key Word Misconceptions

Children often memorize words incorrectly.

### "More" Does Not Always Mean Addition

Example:

Tom has 10 marbles.

He has 3 more marbles than Ben.

How many marbles does Ben have?

Answer:

7

---

### "Left" Does Not Always Mean Subtraction

Understanding the story is more important than spotting keywords.

## Common Misconceptions

### Choosing Operations Based on Keywords

Incorrect:

Always use addition when seeing "more".

Correct:

Understand the relationship.

---

### Ignoring Units

Example:

Answer:

5

Correct:

5 apples

---

### Not Checking Answers

Children may obtain impossible answers without noticing.

---

### Focusing on Numbers Instead of Meaning

Word problems are about situations, not just calculations.
