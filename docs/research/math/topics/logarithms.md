# Logarithms

## Purpose

Help children understand logarithms as the inverse of exponents, building the foundation for algebra, exponential functions, scientific applications, and calculus.

---

## Core Concepts

A logarithm answers the question:

"To what power must the base be raised to obtain a given number?"

Example:

2³ = 8

Therefore:

log₂(8) = 3

Logarithms undo exponents.

Example:

10³ = 1,000

### Stage 2: Exponents and Inverse Operations

Addition ↔ subtraction

Multiplication ↔ division

Exponents ↔ logarithms

### Stage 3: Simple Logarithms

Example:

log₂(8) = 3

---

Example:

log₁₀(100) = 2

---

### Stage 4: Understanding Meaning

Example:

log₅(125)

5 raised to what power equals 125?

## Logarithms as Inverse Functions

Exponential:

2⁴ = 16

Logarithmic:

log₂(16) = 4

---

Exponential:

10³ = 1,000

Logarithmic:

log₁₀(1000) = 3

---

## Common Logarithms

Base:

10

Examples:

---

## Natural Logarithms

Base:

e

Examples:

ln(e) = 1

ln(e²) = 2

## Common Misconceptions

### Logarithms Are Division

Logarithms determine exponents.

---

### Bases Are Optional

Every logarithm has a base.

---

### Logarithms Produce Negative Numbers

Logarithms may be positive, zero, or negative.

---

### Logarithms and Exponents Are Unrelated

They are inverse operations.
