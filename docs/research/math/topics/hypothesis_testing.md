# Hypothesis Testing

## Purpose

Help children understand how evidence can be used to evaluate claims and make decisions under uncertainty, building the foundation for scientific research, statistics, data science, and machine learning.

---

## Core Concepts

Hypothesis testing is a process for deciding whether data provides enough evidence to support a claim.

Instead of proving something with certainty, hypothesis testing evaluates whether observed results are likely or unlikely.

Statistical conclusions involve uncertainty.

## Learning Progression

### Stage 1: Asking Questions

Examples:

- Does a new fertilizer increase plant growth?
- Does studying improve test scores?

Questions lead to hypotheses.

---

### Stage 2: Forming Hypotheses

Null hypothesis:

No effect exists.

Alternative hypothesis:

An effect exists.

---

### Stage 3: Collecting Data

Examples:

- surveys
- experiments
- observations

Evidence comes from data.

---

### Stage 4: Comparing Results

Observed results are compared with expectations.

Unexpected outcomes provide stronger evidence.

---

### Stage 5: Probability and Evidence

Statistics measures how surprising results are.

Highly unusual results may challenge the null hypothesis.

---

### Stage 6: Making Decisions

Possible conclusions:

- fail to reject the null hypothesis
- reject the null hypothesis

---

### Stage 7: Errors

Mistakes are possible.

Conclusions are never absolutely certain.

---

### Stage 8: Scientific Applications

Hypothesis testing helps evaluate claims in science and medicine.

---

## Null Hypothesis

The null hypothesis assumes no difference or no effect.

Example:

A medicine has no effect.

---

## Alternative Hypothesis

The alternative hypothesis suggests that an effect exists.

Example:

A medicine improves recovery.

---

## Evidence

Data provides evidence.

Stronger evidence increases confidence.

---

## P-Values

P-values measure how surprising the observed results are under the null hypothesis.

Smaller p-values indicate stronger evidence against the null hypothesis.

---

Hypothesis testing depends heavily on probability.

## Common Misconceptions

### Hypothesis Testing Proves Things

Statistical conclusions are probabilistic, not absolute.

---

### Large Samples Guarantee Correct Conclusions

Sampling quality also matters.

---

### Rejecting the Null Means Absolute Truth

Evidence supports conclusions but does not guarantee certainty.

---

### Failing to Reject Means the Null Is True

It only means insufficient evidence was found.
