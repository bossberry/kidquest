# Probability

## Purpose

Help children understand chance and uncertainty, building the foundation for statistics, data analysis, scientific reasoning, and decision making.

---

## Core Concepts

Probability describes how likely an event is to happen.

Some events are:

- certain
- likely
- unlikely
- impossible

Probability helps us reason about uncertainty.

## Learning Progression

### Stage 1: Certain and Impossible Events

Examples:

The sun will rise tomorrow.

Certain.

---

A fish will fly like a bird.

Impossible.

Children begin recognizing extremes.

---

### Stage 2: Likely and Unlikely Events

Examples:

Rain tomorrow.

Likely.

---

Winning a lottery.

Unlikely.

Children understand that some events are more probable than others.

---

### Stage 3: Everyday Predictions

Examples:

- weather
- sports games
- traffic conditions

Children learn that predictions involve uncertainty.

---

### Stage 4: Random Experiments

Examples:

- coin flips
- dice rolls
- spinner games

Outcomes are unpredictable.

---

### Stage 5: Equally Likely Outcomes

Example:

A fair coin.

Heads and tails are equally likely.

---

### Stage 6: Listing Possible Outcomes

Example:

Rolling a die.

Possible outcomes:

1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6

Each outcome has the same chance.

---

### Stage 7: Comparing Probabilities

Example:

Bag contains:

3 red balls

1 blue ball

Red is more likely.

Blue is less likely.

---

### Stage 8: Fractions and Probability

Example:

6 balls:

4 red

2 blue

Probability of red:

4/6

Probability of blue:

2/6

Probability connects fractions with chance.

---

## Types of Events

### Certain Events

Probability = 100%

Examples:

Tomorrow follows today.

---

### Impossible Events

Probability = 0%

Examples:

Rolling a 7 on a six-sided die.

---

### Likely Events

More likely to occur.

---

### Unlikely Events

Less likely to occur.

---

### Equally Likely Events

Same probability.

Example:

Heads and tails.

---

## Experimental Probability

Observe actual outcomes.

Example:

Flip a coin 20 times.

Count heads and tails.

Experimental results may vary.

---

## Theoretical Probability

Based on mathematical reasoning.

Example:

Probability of rolling a 3:

1 out of 6

---

## Relationship with Fractions

Probability can be expressed as fractions.

Example:

2 favorable outcomes

8 total outcomes

Probability:

2/8

---

## Relationship with Percentages

Example:

50%

Means:

Half of all outcomes.

---

## Common Misconceptions

### Random Means Patternless

Random events can still produce streaks.

---

### Previous Results Affect Future Results

Coin flips are independent.

Past flips do not change future probabilities.

---

### More Attempts Guarantee Equal Results

Short experiments may vary.

Large numbers tend to balance.

---

### Likely Means Certain

Likely events can still fail to happen.
