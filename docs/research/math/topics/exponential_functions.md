# Exponential Functions

## Purpose

Help children understand patterns of repeated multiplication and rapid growth or decay, building the foundation for logarithms, finance, biology, and advanced mathematics.

---

## Core Concepts

Exponential functions describe situations where quantities change by a constant factor rather than a constant amount.

General form:

y = a·bˣ

where:

- a is the initial value
- b is the growth or decay factor
- x is the input variable

Exponential functions model rapid growth and decay.

## Learning Progression

### Stage 1: Repeated Multiplication

Example:

2, 4, 8, 16, 32

Each term is multiplied by 2.

---

### Stage 2: Comparing with Linear Patterns

Linear:

2, 4, 6, 8, 10

Exponential:

2, 4, 8, 16, 32

Linear patterns add.

Exponential patterns multiply.

---

### Stage 3: Growth Patterns

Example:

1, 2, 4, 8, 16

Growth becomes increasingly rapid.

---

### Stage 4: Decay Patterns

Example:

100, 50, 25, 12.5

Values decrease by a constant factor.

---

### Stage 5: Powers of Two

Examples:

2⁰ = 1

2¹ = 2

2² = 4

2³ = 8

2⁴ = 16

---

### Stage 6: Graphing Exponential Functions

Example:

y = 2ˣ

The graph curves upward.

Growth accelerates.

---

### Stage 7: Exponential Decay

Example:

y = (1/2)ˣ

Values approach zero.

---

### Stage 8: Real-Life Models

Examples:

- population growth
- bacteria growth
- radioactive decay
- compound interest

## Common Misconceptions

### Exponential Growth Means Fast Growth Immediately

Growth becomes faster over time.

---

### Exponential Graphs Are Straight Lines

Exponential graphs are curved.

---

### Exponents Mean Multiplication by the Exponent

Example:

2³ ≠ 2 × 3

2³ = 2 × 2 × 2

---

### Decay Means Negative Numbers

Decay means decreasing values, not negative values.
