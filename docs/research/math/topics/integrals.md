# Integrals

## Purpose

Help children understand accumulation and area, building the foundation for integral calculus, physics, probability, engineering, and advanced mathematics.

---

## Core Concepts

An integral measures accumulation.

Integrals answer questions involving:

- total area
- total distance
- accumulated growth
- quantities built from many small pieces

Integrals are one of the two central ideas of calculus.

## Learning Progression

### Stage 1: Adding Quantities

Examples:

- counting money
- total rainfall
- total distance

Children understand accumulation.

---

### Stage 2: Area Concepts

Area represents the amount of space inside a shape.

Integrals extend area ideas to curved regions.

---

### Stage 3: Approximating with Rectangles

Curved regions may be approximated by many rectangles.

Smaller rectangles produce better approximations.

---

### Stage 4: Definite Integrals

Definite integrals measure total accumulation over an interval.

Example:

Area under a graph between two points.

---

### Stage 5: Indefinite Integrals

Indefinite integrals reverse derivatives.

They are also called antiderivatives.

---

### Stage 6: Relationship with Motion

Velocity accumulates to produce distance.

---

### Stage 7: Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

Derivatives and integrals are inverse processes.

---

### Stage 8: Applications

Integrals describe many physical and scientific phenomena.

---

## Area Under a Curve

Integrals measure area beneath graphs.

Unlike rectangles and triangles, curves require infinitely many tiny pieces.

---

## Accumulation

Examples:

- rainfall over time
- fuel consumption
- population growth

Integrals combine small changes into totals.

---

## Definite Integrals

Definite integrals produce numerical answers.

Example:

Total area from x = 1 to x = 5.

---

## Indefinite Integrals

Indefinite integrals produce functions.

They undo differentiation.

---

## Relationship with Derivatives

Derivative:

Measures change.

Integral:

Measures accumulation.

These processes are inverses.

---

Intervals also matter.
