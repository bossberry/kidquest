# Probability Distributions

## Purpose

Help children understand how probabilities are distributed among possible outcomes, building the foundation for statistics, data science, machine learning, and artificial intelligence.

---

## Core Concepts

A probability distribution describes how likely different outcomes are.

Probability distributions organize uncertainty in a systematic way.

Examples include:

- coin flips
- dice rolls
- heights of people
- test scores

Probability distributions help us understand patterns in randomness.

## Learning Progression

### Stage 1: Review of Probability

Probability measures likelihood.

Examples:

- certain
- likely
- unlikely
- impossible

---

### Stage 2: Multiple Outcomes

Example:

Rolling a die.

Possible outcomes:

1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6

---

### Stage 3: Frequencies

Repeated experiments produce patterns.

Some outcomes occur more frequently than others.

---

### Stage 4: Random Variables

Random variables assign numerical values to outcomes.

Example:

Coin tosses:

Heads = 1

Tails = 0

---

### Stage 5: Probability Tables

Distributions can be organized into tables.

Example:

Outcome | Probability

1 | 1/6

2 | 1/6

...

6 | 1/6

---

### Stage 6: Discrete Distributions

Outcomes are countable.

Examples:

- dice
- cards
- coin flips

---

### Stage 7: Continuous Distributions

Values vary continuously.

Examples:

- heights
- temperatures
- weights

---

### Stage 8: Normal Distribution

Many natural phenomena form a bell-shaped curve.

Examples:

- heights
- IQ scores
- measurement errors

## Common Misconceptions

### Random Means No Pattern

Random processes often produce predictable distributions.

---

### All Distributions Are Bell-Shaped

Many distributions are not normal.

---

### Most Likely Means Guaranteed

High probability does not imply certainty.

---

### Average Means Most Common

Mean and mode are different concepts.
