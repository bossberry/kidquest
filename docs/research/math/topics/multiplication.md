# Multiplication

## Purpose

Help children understand multiplication as repeated addition, equal groups, arrays, and scaling, laying the foundation for division and algebra.

---

## Core Concepts

Multiplication means:

- repeated addition
- equal groups
- arrays
- scaling quantities

Example:

3 × 4

means

3 groups of 4

4 + 4 + 4 = 12

Answer:

12

## Learning Progression

### Stage 1: Repeated Addition

Example:

2 + 2 + 2

can be written as

3 × 2

Answer:

6

---

### Stage 2: Equal Groups

Example:

4 baskets

Each basket has 3 apples.

🍎🍎🍎

🍎🍎🍎

🍎🍎🍎

Answer:

12 apples

---

### Stage 3: Arrays

Example:

3 rows of 4

● ● ● ●

● ● ● ●

● ● ● ●

Answer:

12

Arrays help children visualize multiplication.

---

### Stage 4: Skip Counting

Count by:

2s

5s

10s

Examples:

2, 4, 6, 8, 10

5, 10, 15, 20

10, 20, 30, 40

---

### Stage 5: Multiplication Facts

Examples:

1 × 5 = 5

2 × 6 = 12

4 × 3 = 12

7 × 8 = 56

Children gradually develop fluency.

---

### Stage 6: Commutative Property

Example:

3 × 4 = 4 × 3

Order does not change the answer.

---

### Stage 7: Scaling

Example:

One toy costs 20 baht.

Three toys cost:

3 × 20 = 60 baht

## Properties of Multiplication

### Identity Property

7 × 1 = 7

---

### Zero Property

9 × 0 = 0

---

### Commutative Property

2 × 5 = 5 × 2

---

### Associative Property

(2 × 3) × 4

=

2 × (3 × 4)

---

## Relationship with Division

Multiplication and division are inverse operations.

Example:

4 × 3 = 12

12 ÷ 3 = 4

12 ÷ 4 = 3

---

## Common Misconceptions

### Multiplication Always Makes Numbers Bigger

Incorrect:

0.5 × 8

Answer:

4

Multiplication can sometimes make quantities smaller.

---

### Confusing Factors and Products

Example:

3 × 5

Factors:

3 and 5

Product:

15

---

### Counting Groups Incorrectly

Children may confuse:

3 groups of 4

with

4 groups of 3

Although both equal 12, the situations are different.

## Teacher Notes

Begin with concrete objects and arrays.

Avoid teaching multiplication tables through memorization alone.

Build conceptual understanding first.

---

## Parent Notes

Use everyday situations:

- eggs in cartons
- chairs at tables
- snacks in bags
- toys in groups

Ask:

"How many groups?"

"How many in each group?"
